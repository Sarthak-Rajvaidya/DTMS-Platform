Module 2: Earthquake Safety & Survival Guidelines

The "Proactive Survivor" Framework

Estimated: 45 min • Practical Survival Skills • 5 Sections

Module Progress 0% Complete

Section 1: Preparation (Before the Shaking)

Effective survival begins months before the fault line slips

The Proactive Survivor Mindset

Don't wait for the ground to shake. Prepare today for tomorrow's safety.

The "3 AM Walkthrough" Drill

Simulate Complete Darkness

Navigate your home in total darkness. Any furniture you trip over or anything that falls on your bed in this drill is a potential hazard during a real quake.

Test paths: From bed to door, from couch to shelter
Identify hazards: Unsecured shelves, hanging objects, glass furniture
Go-Bag Essentials

Beyond basic food and water, your emergency kit must include:

  • Whistle: To alert rescuers without wasting breath
  • Manual tools: Wrench/pliers for gas shut-off
  • Sturdy work gloves: Protect hands from debris
Home Hardening

Secure your space before disaster strikes:

  • L-brackets: Anchor tall furniture to wall studs
  • Museum Wax/Adhesive Putty: Secure smaller valuables on shelves
  • Safety film: Apply to windows to prevent glass shattering
Critical Check: The Bedroom

Your bedroom is where you're most vulnerable at night. Ensure nothing heavy is above your bed, and keep sturdy shoes and a flashlight within arm's reach.

Section 2: Immediate Actions (During the Shaking)

Reaction time is everything - you have 3-10 seconds

3-10 Second Window

You have roughly 3–10 seconds before the most violent waves arrive after the initial tremor.

The "Drop, Cover, and Hold On" Protocol

Drop

Lower your center of gravity to prevent being knocked down

Cover

Protect head and neck with sturdy table or desk

Hold On

Grip table leg - furniture moves during quakes

Location-Specific Scenarios
🏢 High-Rise Office

Stay away from windows. The building is designed to sway; do not rush for stairs or elevators.

🛒 Supermarket/Retail

Use shopping cart as head shield. Move away from glass storefronts and heavy shelving.

🎬 Cinema/Stadium

Stay in your seat. Lean forward, protect head. Do not exit until shaking stops to avoid stampede.

🚗 Vehicle

Pull over, set parking brake. Stay inside. Avoid overpasses, bridges, power lines, and signs.

The "Triangle of Life" Warning

Avoid the "Triangle of Life" myth (hiding next to, rather than under, furniture). Leading global agencies (Red Cross, FEMA) advise that under a table is much safer from falling ceiling debris. This myth has been repeatedly debunked by earthquake safety experts.

Section 3: Points of Critical Awareness

Understanding the "invisible" dangers

Beyond the Shaking: Hidden Hazards

The "Glass Rain" Effect

In urban centers, skyscraper windows often shatter and fall blocks away from the building.

Action: If outside during shaking, do not look up. Duck into a building's reinforced lobby or seek shelter in a doorway.
Soil Liquefaction

Shaking can turn solid, sandy soil into a liquid-like state, causing buildings to sink or tilt.

Action: If you see "sand boils" (mud bubbling from the ground), the area is structurally unstable. Move to rocky or higher ground immediately.
The Aftershock Cycle

The first quake is rarely the last. Aftershocks can happen:

Minutes Later
Immediate danger period
Days Later
Weakened structures collapse
Months Later
Continuing seismic activity

Warning: Aftershocks often collapse buildings that were only "weakened" by the first quake.

Section 4: The Hierarchy of Rescue

Self First, Others Next - The "Oxygen Mask" Principle

The "Oxygen Mask" Principle

You cannot help others if you become a victim yourself. Secure your safety first.

Phase 1: Save Yourself (First 60 Seconds)

Neutralize Space: If cooking, turn off stove only if it's one step away
Armor Up: Put on sturdy shoes immediately after shaking stops
Self-Check: Adrenaline masks pain. Check limbs for blood, check pulse before moving objects

Phase 2: Secure Your "Safe Base" (Minutes 2–10)

The 360-Check: Scan ceiling for cracks, floor for glass, air for smells
The Gas Protocol: If you smell "rotten eggs," open window and leave immediately
Critical Warning: Never flip a light switch or use a cell phone near a gas leak—a single spark can trigger an explosion.

Phase 3: Saving Others (The Triage Method)

Voice Search: Call out: "I am here, can anyone hear me?" Listen for tapping or shouts
The "Easy Wins": Help conscious, lightly trapped people first to build rescue team
Medical Priority: Focus on "Stop the Bleed." Use pressure or tourniquet on heavy limb bleeding
The "Crush Syndrome" Warning

Critical: If a victim has been pinned under a heavy beam for more than 15-20 minutes, do not move the beam without professional medics. Releasing the pressure can send built-up toxins into their heart, causing immediate death.

Phase 4: Collective Response

Text, Don't Talk: Phone lines must remain open for emergency calls. Send one "I'm Safe" text and get off network
Mark the House: Once a room is searched and empty, mark door with "X" using tape or chalk

This tells professional rescuers not to waste time re-searching that space.

Section 5: The 72-Hour Timeline

Help is local - Be prepared to be your own first responder

The 72-Hour Reality

Professional help may take up to 3 days to reach you in a major disaster. Your preparedness determines your survival.

Day 1: Immediate Survival (0-24 Hours)

Fire Suppression

Extinguish small fires immediately. Know location of fire extinguishers.

Immediate First Aid

Treat life-threatening injuries. Stop bleeding, clear airways.

Securing Perimeter

Check for structural damage. Secure safe exit routes. Shut off utilities if necessary.

Day 2: Sustaining Life (24-48 Hours)

Water Rationing

Implement strict water rationing. Minimum 1 liter per person per day.

Sanitation & Hygiene

Establish sanitation area. Prevent disease spread with proper waste disposal.

Community Check

Check on vulnerable neighbors. Pool resources with nearby survivors.

Day 3: Planning Forward (48-72 Hours)

Information Gathering

Listen for official government broadcasts regarding relief efforts.

AM Radio Battery Radio Emergency Alerts
Resource Location

Identify food/water distribution points. Plan safe routes to these locations.

Water Stations Medical Tents Food Distribution
Remember: After the initial 72 hours, the transition to recovery begins. Your preparedness during these critical days determines your long-term survival and ability to help others.
